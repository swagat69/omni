Show Notes

Why Does This Episode Matter?

Scaling offline stores sounds simple until you are actually doing it — and suddenly 100 stores feel harder to manage than 10 ever did. Traffic patterns differ, staff performance varies, conversion drops in ways you cannot explain, and the data you need to fix it simply does not exist.

Suren has been in the middle of that chaos with some of India's leading retail brands, and he has built the playbook to navigate it. In this conversation, he breaks down how tech — especially visual AI and in-store analytics — can give brands the visibility and control they need to scale fast without losing quality.

"Stores consistently under-report their own footfall. Because when the security guard is counting, they're working backwards from the conversion number they already achieved. So the count always conveniently matches. You're not optimizing for potential — you're just justifying outcomes."

What Is Tango Eye?

The simplest way to describe Tango Eye is that it's Google Analytics for your physical store. It plugs into your existing CCTV cameras — no new hardware needed — and runs an intelligence layer that tells you what's actually happening inside your store.

Tango Eye currently operates across 10,000+ stores, 50–60 brands, and multiple countries including India, Singapore, and the UAE.

What Does the Offline Conversion Funnel Really Look Like?

Think of footfall like website visits. Out of 100 people who walk in:

  • 10 are staff — remove them
  • 20 are "bouncers" — people asking where the restroom is, not potential buyers
  • You're left with 70. But families walk in together and share one bill, so your true individual potential buyers might be closer to 40

Now you have 40 genuine prospects. What happens to them inside the store — which aisle they visit, how long they spend, whether a staff member approaches them — that's your conversion funnel. And that's where Tango Eye lives.

One brand Suren mentioned tracks it even deeper. A Gen Z fast fashion brand measures store-to-trial-room conversion first, then trial-room-to-purchase. If people are trying stuff but not buying, that's a product problem. If they're not making it to the trial room, that's a store experience problem. Very different fixes.

Operational Tech vs Customer Tech

When we talk about tech in an offline store, it breaks down into two clear sets — Operational Tech that powers the business behind the scenes, and Customer Tech that shapes the experience on the floor.

Before the fancy customer-facing tech, there's a whole layer of basic operational tech that most people underestimate:

  • Staff attendance and monitoring — one brand had a conversion problem in the evenings. After tracking data, they discovered the last two hours were covered by one person, during the highest footfall window on weekends. That's a scheduling problem.
  • Process checklists — from store opening procedures to cash management to pricing cards. When brands are small, founders handle this personally. As they scale, consistency disappears unless it's systematized.
  • Footfall + demographics — one brand trying to scale its kids' collection found which stores actually had families walking in, and doubled down on kids' inventory there.

Customer-Facing Tech Worth Investing In

  • Virtual try-on — eyewear brands have done it for years. The Pant Project built something that shows how a product looks and helps figure out sizing and fit.
  • Endless aisle — a QR code or screen in-store that lets you browse the full catalogue. Decathlon does this well. If your size or colour isn't available, scan it, order it, get it delivered. You don't lose the sale.
  • Appointment booking — works brilliantly for high-touch categories: jewelry, eyewear, wedding wear. Signals high-intent customers in advance.
  • Self-checkout — growing in large format retail. Decathlon and Uniqlo have redesigned the staff role entirely. One person can manage multiple checkout points.

The Biggest Mistake Brands Make

Brands spend months comparing, piloting, and trying tech. Then they compile it all into a presentation that nobody acts on.

The brands getting the most out of operational and store tech are the ones treating the data as a daily operational input, not a quarterly report.

Where Is AI Taking Store Tech Next?

We're moving from AI as a data collector to AI as a decision-maker.

  • How many staff should this store have on Saturday evening?
  • What products should go in the front aisle for the next two weeks?
  • Where should the new collection launch first, based on customer demographics by location?

These decisions are now being assisted by AI — and the gap between brands that use this well and those that don't is going to widen fast.

The Biggest Takeaway

Offline retail is not dying — it is just finally getting the analytics layer it always deserved. Most store problems are scheduling, merchandising, or compliance problems in disguise. The brands scaling from 10 stores to 500 are not spending more — they are simply seeing more clearly than everyone else.
